## Atmospheric Reality Gap

Both hero images attached show the same frame — identical photographs submitted twice. That's the most expensive visual signal on your homepage: you are recycling a single asset and presenting it as a library. The image itself is technically competent. Light is flat but even, focus lands on the foreground subject, and the BOSU-ball group formation communicates coached effort. What it does not communicate is *Olympia*. There is no facility-specific detail — no recognizable court, no pool deck, no equipment floor — nothing that separates this frame from a stock-library search result for "group fitness class." Your copy promises "state-of-the-art gym equipment, four indoor tennis courts, a heated pool" — that's three distinct environments. Your hero delivers one generic studio wall. The trust cost: a prospect comparing you against a competitor who shows their actual facility reads your homepage and has no visual proof you are what you claim to be. Doubt costs you the inquiry before the tour popup ever opens.

## Visual Narrative Coherence

Three of your 20 homepage links are broken, and all three are high-intent destinations. The Member Portal at `staclife.clubautomation.com` returns a 403 — meaning any current member clicking to log in hits a wall. That's not a prospect problem; that's a retention problem. Your Facebook link returns a 400, severing the social proof loop you need to validate the testimonials section sitting one scroll above it. Your Yelp listing at `yelp.com/biz/steamboat-tennis-and-athletic-club-olympia` returns a 403 — the exact page a skeptical buyer opens in a second tab to verify your member satisfaction claims. A high-value buyer running this comparison sees a gym that cannot maintain its own links and concludes: if they can't keep the door open on Yelp, what else is deferred? The broken Member Portal link also means "BOOK A TOUR" is doing conversion work that a functional member-facing portal should be reinforcing. You're bleeding on both ends simultaneously.

## Metadata and Conversion Gaps

Your alt-text coverage is 0% missing across 11 content images — that infrastructure is clean and you should keep it. The filenames on your newer images follow a descriptive convention (`steamboat_tennis_and_athletic_club_womans_exercise_on_floor.webp`, `steamboat_tennis_and_athletic_club_girl_play_tennis.webp`) which feeds Google Image search with location-relevant terms. That is working in your favor and should be the standard for every upload going forward.

The findability problem is in your page title. At 76 characters it clips in most SERPs, and more critically, it leads with a subjective superlative — "The Best Gym and Tennis Club in Olympia" — rather than a search-intent phrase that a new resident or visitor would actually type. You rank #1 for your brand name, which means people who already know you can find you. The buyers who don't know you yet — searching "gym near Olympia" or "indoor tennis courts Olympia WA" — are landing on whoever holds those non-branded positions. Your title and meta description (170 characters) are doing nothing to compete for that unconverted traffic. One new membership per month from organic non-branded search at your membership price point is a real number forfeited to a title tag written for brand confirmation instead of acquisition.

The "BOOK A TOUR" CTA appears three times on the homepage, but the nav-level instance links to `staclife.com` — the homepage itself — not the popup or a dedicated landing page. You are sending ready-to-act visitors back to the page they are already on.
